3(x) + 1

The Collatz conjecture is a mathematical conjecture that states that for any positive integer, the following process will eventually reach the number 1: if the integer is even, divide it by 2, and if the integer is odd, multiply it by 3 and add 1; named after Lothar Collatz, the German mathematician who first proposed it in 1937.

The conjecture is simple to state, but it has demonstrated to be very difficult to prove or disprove. Despite being easy to compute, it's not well understood, no proof or a counterexample has been found, and is still considered an open problem in mathematics.

It has been tested extensively for many integers and has been found that the conjecture holds for all integers up to 5*10^18; However, this does not prove the conjecture for all integers, as there may be integers for which the conjecture does not hold.

You may sometimes see it called the 3x+1 problem, because of the rule that a number is multiplied by 3 and increased by 1 if it is odd.

The conjecture has been the subject of much research and study, and there have been several proposed proofs and attempted proofs that have not been accepted as valid. Despite this, it is true for all integers up to 5*10^18.

It is also worth noting that this conjecture is related to the study of dynamical systems and the behavior of the iterated function in the long term. The conjecture is an example of a problem that is simple to state, but difficult to solve, and it is considered a challenging problem in number theory and mathematics.
